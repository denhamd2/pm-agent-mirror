## Executive Summary

India combines a **large and fast-growing HR technology market**, **extreme digital and mobile penetration**, and **structurally high-volume hiring** (IT services, GCCs, manufacturing-linked job creation) with a **new national data protection regime** (Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 and DPDP Rules 2025) that reframes consent, vendor governance, and cross-border transfers. Political direction favours **formalisation of work** (labour code implementation, gig and platform worker social security) and **industrial scale-up** (PLI-linked employment), which increases compliance surface area for enterprise ATS and onboarding journeys.

For **Know Your Candidate**, the macro picture is decisive: employers remain **data fiduciaries** with **granular consent and minimisation duties**, while **private-sector use of Aadhaar for routine employment verification was curtailed** by the Supreme Court in 2018 (Section 57 struck down), so product strategy must assume **non-Aadhaar-first** identity and verification patterns, strong **BGV vendor DPAs**, and **auditable automation** where AI or high-volume matching is used. **EU GDPR** and the **EU AI Act** still matter for Workday’s **multinational customers** and **EU-origin candidate data** transferred to India or processed in global tenants: there is **no EU adequacy decision for India**, so **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** and **transfer impact assessments** remain the default bridge.

**Environmental** factors are secondary for core recruiting SKU design but **BRSR-listed employers** continue to disclose workforce and inclusion themes, nudging larger customers toward **reportable diversity and well-being metrics** that recruiting data can support upstream.

## 01. Political Factor

India’s centre continues to push **labour law consolidation** (four codes replacing a large set of central statutes) and **implementation in phases**, affecting contracts, fixed-term employment parity, social security, and compliance tooling expectations for employers. Commentary through 2025–2026 highlights **wage structure impacts** (for example, allowances capped relative to wages under the Code on Wages narrative), **policy rewrites**, and **investment in compliance technology** as firms operationalise the new framework. The **Industrial Relations Code, 2020** reframes standing orders, dispute resolution, and union recognition, which matters less to ATS configuration than to **downstream HCM and workforce policy** alignment for integrated suite buyers.

**Gig and platform work** gained clearer statutory recognition under the **Code on Social Security, 2020**, with **aggregator contributions** to social security funds and **e-Shram**-style registration narratives emphasising portability; this signals growing **government interest in non-traditional employment** and may expand the types of hiring programmes (contract, gig, partner workforces) that enterprises run on a single people platform.

**Industrial policy** remains a jobs lever: government releases through late 2025 cite **PLI schemes** attracting very large cumulative **investment** and **direct and indirect employment** in the millions of jobs range across sectors such as electronics, autos, and pharma, reinforcing **high-volume hiring waves** in manufacturing and supply chains alongside services.

**Strategic posture** for global vendors: **data sovereignty and trusted cloud** narratives are prominent in **MeitY**-aligned policy discussion (empanelled cloud, AI compute tenders with security and localisation emphasis), reinforcing customer expectations that **sensitive workloads can be hosted or controlled in India** where required.

**Key findings**

• Labour code rollout increases **HR policy and payroll restructuring** work for customers; recruiting must stay aligned with **offer, contract type, and workforce category** semantics used downstream.  
• Platform and gig recognition raises **volume and variety** of engagement types that TA teams source, screen, and onboard.  
• PLI and GCC growth narratives support **sustained bulk hiring** and **tier-2/3 expansion**, shaping need for **scale automation** without sacrificing auditability.

Other analyst headlines cluster around similar **USD ~1.2B+** current scale and **high single-digit CAGR** through the early 2030s, with **recruitment** often cited as a leading application segment (including shares in the **mid-twenties percent** range in trade summaries).

**High-volume hiring** is visible in **IT and GCC** narratives: industry reporting points to **large fresher intakes** in FY26 versus FY25, **campus hiring recovery**, and **GCC hiring growth** in percentage terms, with **AI, cloud, and cybersecurity** roles called out. **GCC** ecosystems are described as employing **well over a million professionals** with **hundreds of thousands** of annual job creation in some estimates, reinforcing **enterprise TA throughput** as a norm, not an edge case.

**Workday’s India footprint** (public messaging, 2025) stresses **local data centre** availability on **AWS in India** for core modules and **continued headcount and office expansion**, which signals **market commitment** and **data placement** flexibility for buyers.

## 03. Social Factor

India’s **digital baseline** is exceptionally strong for **mobile-first candidate journeys**. DataReportal (late 2025, published as **Digital 2026: India**) reports **1.03 billion internet users** (**70.0% penetration**), **1.06 billion cellular mobile connections** (**72.5% of population**, with caveats on multi-SIM and non-data plans), and **500 million social media identities** (**34.1% of population**). UN population reference used in the same report: **1.47 billion** people, **median age 28.8**, underscoring a **young, digitally reachable** workforce.

**Professional graph** reach: DataReportal cites **LinkedIn** advertising resources showing **170 million members** in India (late 2025), **16.6% of the local internet user base**, with strong year-on-year audience growth in ad planning tools. **Facebook** ad reach **403 million**, **Snapchat** **213 million**, and **Messenger** **108 million** illustrate **platform fragmentation**; for recruiting, **LinkedIn plus mobile messaging** is the practical combination for many employers.

**WhatsApp** is widely treated as the **default business and candidate messaging layer** in India; trade and vendor blogs cite **hundreds of millions** of users and **high open/response rates** versus email, plus **API adoption growth** for SMBs and enterprises. These figures are **directionally consistent** but **less uniformly documented** than DataReportal’s platform-specific pages; treat **WhatsApp as socially dominant** while citing **messaging performance claims** as **industry guidance**, not government statistics.

PIB-referenced household survey material (2025) has been cited in secondary reporting for **very high smartphone ownership among younger cohorts** in urban and rural splits, reinforcing **mobile application and messaging** as primary UX assumptions.

## 04. Technological Factor

Indian enterprises are among the **most aggressive adopters of AI at scale** in global surveys. Deloitte’s **State of AI in the enterprise 2026** India insights (press release, March 2026) state **40%** of Indian respondents report **significant or full AI usage** versus **~28% global**, with strongest at-scale deployment in **product development (62%)**, **strategy and operations (56%)**, **marketing and sales (55%)**, and **supply chain (48%)**. **94%** expect **AI spend to increase**; **68%** prioritise **security and compliance controls** for scaling AI. **Regulatory and compliance requirements** are the **top integration challenge (39%)**.

Parallel labour-market reporting (for example ICRIER via press coverage, 2026) notes **AI shifting junior hiring intensity** while **demand for hybrid AI-plus-domain skills** rises, which affects **volume and skill filters** in recruiting funnels.

**Cloud and data centre** capacity narratives project **multi-fold growth** in MW capacity through 2030; **MeitY**-style **empanelment** and **AI compute** programmes seek **secure, localised** options for academia, startups, and government, reinforcing **sovereign cloud** demand.

**Key findings**

• AI adoption **ahead of governance maturity** (Deloitte: **0–4%** high-level AI expertise in India vs **2–8%** global band in the same release).  
• Compliance and security are **primary AI enablers** in buyer minds, not optional extras.  
• Infrastructure growth supports **SaaS latency and in-country hosting** expectations.

## 06. Legal & Compliance Factor

### Data protection and privacy (India, primary)

India’s **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP Act)** establishes **Data Fiduciaries** and **Data Principals**, grounds including **consent** and **certain legitimate uses** (see **Section 7** narratives in legal commentary), duties of **accuracy, security, and breach notification**, and a **Data Protection Board of India** enforcement layer. **DPDP Rules, 2025** operationalise notices, consent mechanics, **children’s data**, **grievance handling**, and **cross-border flows**; commentary tracks **phased effective dates**, with **Rule 15 (cross-border transfer)** widely discussed as **effective May 2027** in guide summaries (verify against your legal counsel for each obligation’s exact clock).

**Penalties** are **material**: statutory frames cite **up to ₹250 crore** for certain serious failures (for example security safeguards and breach handling in tier tables), with **lower bands** for other breaches; the Board may consider **gravity, duration, repetitive nature**, and **remediation**.

**Implications for Know Your Candidate**

• **Consent**: Pre-employment **background verification** is **high-risk for “bundled” consent**; legal commentary urges **specific, informed, separable** consent and **DPAs with processors** (BGV vendors).  
• **Minimisation and retention**: Collect **only necessary** verification fields; align **retention** to **clear schedules**; support **erasure and correction** workflows for data principals.  
• **Processors**: Flow-down obligations to **screening, identity, and document vendors** with **sub-processor transparency**.

**Aadhaar and identity**

The **Supreme Court (September 2018)** upheld most of the **Aadhaar Act, 2016** but **struck down Section 57**, ending **private-sector mandatory Aadhaar** as a general gate. Private employers should **not assume Aadhaar-based eKYC** as default; **Know Your Candidate** flows should support **lawful, proportionate alternatives** (document-based checks, vendor KYC, education and employment verification) consistent with **DPDP** and sector practice.

**Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDF)**

Where notified, SDFs face **additional duties**: **Data Protection Officer in India**, **periodic DPIAs**, **independent audits**, and **review of automated processing** to address **bias and unfair outcomes** (see Section 10 family of obligations in commentaries). Large **high-volume recruiting** platforms and enterprises could fall in scope depending on **government notification**, so product architecture should **not preclude** SDF-grade **logging, DPIA artefacts, and human oversight**.

### Cross-border transfers (India outbound)

DPDP commentary describes a **“negative list” / restricted-territory** model under **Rule 15** rather than case-by-case approval for every transfer; **government notifications** can change **permitted destinations**. Organisations must still align **consent and notice** with **transfer** and maintain **grievance redressal** for principals regardless of storage location.

### EU GDPR and EU AI Act (secondary, for multinational and EU data subjects)

Where **EU/EEA personal data** is processed for **offering services** or **monitoring behaviour** in the Union, **Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR)** applies. Recruiting-relevant anchors include:

• **Article 5**: principles including lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity and confidentiality.  
• **Article 6**: lawful basis for processing (often **legitimate interests** or **consent** for candidates).  
• **Article 9**: **special category** data; explicit consent or narrow exceptions for diversity and health-related recruiting data.  
• **Articles 13–14**: **transparent information** to candidates at collection.  
• **Article 17**: **erasure** and candidate lifecycle endings.  
• **Article 20**: **data portability** where technically feasible.  
• **Article 22**: rights related to **solely automated decisions** with legal or similarly significant effects, including **human intervention**.  
• **Article 35**: **DPIA** where high-risk processing (for example extensive profiling or sensitive-scale screening).  
• **Articles 44–50**: **international transfers** (SCCs, TIA/DTIA, supplementary measures).  
• **Article 83**: **penalties** up to **€20 million or 4% of global annual turnover** (whichever higher) for serious infringements.

**India–EU adequacy**: As of early 2026 commentary, **no EU adequacy decision** is in force for India; transfers typically rely on **SCCs** plus **assessment** (see EDPB guidance on international transfers).

**Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (EU AI Act)** classifies **AI for recruitment and selection** as **high-risk** where covered (**Annex III**). For in-scope deployments in the EU, expect **risk management (Article 9)**, **data governance (Article 10)**, **technical documentation (Article 11)**, **record-keeping (Article 12)**, **transparency to deployers (Article 13)**, **human oversight (Article 14)**, and **Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (Article 27)** where applicable, with **registration** timelines for high-risk systems in the **2027** horizon per public summaries. **Article 5** prohibits certain practices (for example **emotion inference in workplace** in specified conditions). Align **HiredScore**-style ranking with **human-in-the-loop** UX and **candidate disclosure**.

Official reference hubs: [GDPR full text and articles](https://gdpr-info.eu/) and [EU AI Act explorer](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/ai-act-explorer/).

### Labour, equality, and workplace law touchpoints

• **Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016**: **reservation** and **non-discrimination** duties in **government** contexts; **reasonable accommodation** themes increasingly litigated; **Supreme Court** (2026) commentary rejects **arbitrary disability ceilings** where capability exists.  
• **Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 (POSH)**: **employer duties** to safe workplace, **Internal Committee**, awareness; applies with **>10 workers**; shapes **onboarding and policy** ecosystems tied to hiring.  
• **Contract labour and principal–contractor** dynamics remain structurally important in India; ATS should not **encode illegal pass-through** practices but should support **transparent worker category** tracking as customers require.

**Enforcement landscape**

• **India**: **Data Protection Board of India** as DPDP enforcer; **expect active first-wave cases** as May 2027-class deadlines approach.  
• **EU**: **DPAs** under GDPR; **AI Act** oversight structure phasing through designated authorities.

**Product implication (minimum 50 words)**

Workday Recruiting must ship **India-credible privacy and verification**: **granular consent and notices** for **candidate and BGV data**, **processor and sub-processor governance**, **retention and deletion** aligned to **DPDP**, and **identity paths that do not depend on mandatory Aadhaar** for private-sector hiring after **2018**. For **high-volume** and **AI-assisted matching**, implement **auditable decisions**, **human review affordances**, and **DPIA-ready artefacts** for customers who are or may become **Significant Data Fiduciaries**. For **EU data subjects** and **inbound EU transfers to India**, preserve **GDPR** bases, **Articles 13–14 transparency**, **Article 17/20 rights tooling**, **Article 22** safeguards for automation, and **Articles 44–50 transfer mechanisms** (typically **SCCs** with **DTIA**). ## Cross-Factor Insights

**Political scale-up** (PLI, GCC, labour formalisation) and **economic HR tech growth** converge on **throughput**: Indian enterprise TA is often **campaign-style**, **multi-channel**, and **tier-2 distributed**, which stresses **identity resolution** and **duplicate management** more than in slower markets. **Social mobile-first** behaviour amplifies **messaging-led recruiting**, which must be reconciled with **DPDP consent** and **EU-grade transparency** for global employers. **Technological AI enthusiasm** runs **ahead of governance maturity**, so **Know Your Candidate** features that **accelerate verification** must also **reduce legal surface area** (minimisation, vendor DPAs, human oversight). **EU GDPR and AI Act** remain **parallel compliance rails** for the same SKUs when customers operate **cross-border** or **EU entities**.

---

## Strategic Implications for Workday Recruiting

• **New market entry**: Lead with **in-country hosting narrative**, **DPDP-ready consent and rights**, and **high-volume reliability** (performance, batch, dedupe).  
• **Know Your Candidate**: Prioritise **non-Aadhaar-first identity**, **BGV orchestration with lawful consent**, **document authenticity and deduplication**, and **explainable matching** with **human checkpoints**.  
• **AI**: Position **HiredScore** as **governed acceleration**; pre-package **disclosure templates** and **review queues** that satisfy **DPDP SDF review** themes and **EU AI Act** human oversight expectations.  
• **Channels**: Align candidate messaging to **LinkedIn + mobile messaging** realities; route **WhatsApp** via **approved partner patterns** consistent with customer policies.
