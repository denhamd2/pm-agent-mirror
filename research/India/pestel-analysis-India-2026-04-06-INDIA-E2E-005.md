## Executive summary

India’s macro-environment for enterprise recruiting technology in 2026 combines **operational scale** (large IT/GCC and services hiring waves, heavy use of RPO and campus pipelines), **channel reality** (WhatsApp- and mobile-first candidate journeys), and **trust risk** (resume discrepancy narratives, forged documents, and **deepfake** attempts in remote screening). Politically, **central labour code implementation** (notified effective **21 November 2025** in widely reported commentary) tightens documentation, principal-employer duties for contract labour, and social-security formalisation for **gig and platform workers**, increasing the digital trace employers expect from HR systems. Economically, analyst bands size **India HR technology** toward roughly **USD 1.2B+ in 2025** with **high-single-digit CAGR** through the early 2030s (publisher-dependent), while **recruitment software** is a distinct, smaller sub-segment with strong **SaaS** mix. Socially, **rising female labour-force participation** (PLFS and Economic Survey narratives), **Gen AI skills** pressure on hiring (LinkedIn-surveyed recruiter behaviour), and **transgender workplace equality** litigation and **2025 rules** in some states raise expectations for fair, explainable processes. Technologically, **cloud HCM** adoption, **AI-assisted screening**, and **identity/offline verification** tooling are mainstream investment areas; MeitY’s **October 2025** IT Rules amendments add **synthetic-media labelling** duties on intermediaries that sit adjacent to how candidates and platforms share media. Environmentally, **BRSR** and corporate sustainability reporting drive **green skills** and workforce transition narratives more than pre-hire recruiting compliance, though listed employers increasingly disclose workforce and well-being metrics. Legally, **DPDP Act 2023** plus **DPDP Rules 2025** (notified **November 2025** per government and advisory summaries) move India to a **consent- and notice-centric** regime with **Data Protection Board** penalties (tiered **up to ₹250 crore** for certain contraventions per published Section 33 schedules), **cross-border** flows on a **restrictable default** (negative-list style per Rule 15 commentary), and **Significant Data Fiduciary** duties for large processors. Multinationals remain exposed to **GDPR** (e.g. **Article 22** limitations on solely automated decisions; **Articles 5, 6, 9, 13–14, 17, 20, 35, 44–50, 83**) and the **EU AI Act** (**Annex III(4)(a)** captures recruitment and candidate evaluation AI; **Articles 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 27** structure provider/deployer obligations) for EU data subjects and EU deployers.

**Product headline:** Workday Recruiting’s India story should fuse **high-throughput, audit-ready hiring** with **DPDP-aligned consent, notice, retention, and vendor governance**, **identity and document integrity** (without over-collecting Aadhaar where minimisation suffices), **WhatsApp-grade engagement**, and **AI plus human oversight** that satisfies **EU AI Act** and **GDPR** overlays for global accounts.

## 01. Political factor

**Labour code implementation and employer compliance load**

- The **four Labour Codes** (Wages, Industrial Relations, Occupational Safety, Social Security) are widely reported as **notified effective 21 November 2025**, consolidating **29** prior central laws; commentary emphasises **immediate** compliance expectations for employers, with **state rules** still rolling out (advisory summaries note continued reliance on non-conflicting legacy rules until state adoption).  
- **Contract labour and principal employers:** Guidance summaries highlight **principal employer** liability expansion for contract-worker entitlements (e.g. gratuity narratives moving toward **one year** of service in commentary), **mandatory appointment letters** for broader worker categories, and tighter **holiday/leave** alignment in contractor agreements.  
- **Gig and platform workers:** The **Social Security Code, 2020** framework (notified with the wider code package in late-2025 commentary) formalises registration, aggregator reporting, and benefit scaffolding; **Karnataka** has a **2025** state-level platform-worker welfare law and rules, signalling **state divergence** employers must track.

**Digital government and identity-adjacent policy**

- **Aadhaar** ecosystem updates (**Authentication and Offline Verification Amendment Regulations, 2025**) continue to shape **e-KYC** and **offline verification** options; **ESIC** use of Aadhaar for benefits has seen **Labour Ministry** clarification that authentication is **not universally mandatory** for beneficiaries in reported **August 2025** commentary, underscoring **voluntariness** themes that still bump against operational KYC habits.

**Stability and reform direction**

- Policy direction favours **formalisation** of work, **social security expansion**, and **digital traceability**. That increases **board and GC scrutiny** of recruiting data, contractor hiring, and platform-sourced labour.

**Key findings**

- Recruiting and TA platforms are downstream of **labour-code compliance** (contracts, letters, social security identifiers), not isolated “ATS-only” purchases.  
- **State plus central** layers mean **multi-entity Indian employers** need configurable policy and reporting, not a single national template.

## 02. Economic factor

**Market size and growth (HR tech and recruitment software)**

- **IMARC**-style estimates cited in industry summaries put **India human resource technology** at roughly **USD 1,210M in 2025** toward **USD 2,440M by 2034** at ~**7.6–7.7% CAGR** (segment definitions vary by publisher).  
- **India recruitment software** is sized smaller (analyst summaries cite roughly **USD 81.6M in 2025** toward **USD 122.9M by 2034**, **~4.5% CAGR** in one IMARC segment view), with **~70% SaaS** deployment and **large enterprise** share concentration in the same summaries.

**High-volume hiring and industry structure**

- **IT services** campus and lateral hiring remains a **volume bellwether**: press summaries cite **TCS, Infosys, Wipro** collectively targeting on the order of **~72,000 freshers in FY26**, with **Cognizant** and **Deloitte** publishing large India hiring intent figures in trade press (**25,000** fresher-scale and **50,000** professional-scale narratives respectively).  
- **NASSCOM** strategic commentary positions **India tech revenue** toward **~USD 315B by FY26** with **~135,000 net headcount** additions, and highlights **GCC** growth (**1,750+** GCCs) and **AI-led** productivity shifts that change hiring **mix** versus raw volume.  
- **RPO** commentary sizes India near **INR 8,000–9,000 crore (~USD 1.0–1.1B) in 2025** with **high-teens CAGR**, with **IT/ITES, BFSI, GCCs** as core segments, implying many enterprise ATS processes are **co-managed** by outsourcers.

**Competitive economic dynamic**

- **Suite and cloud** vendors compete on **payroll/statutory adjacency** and **time-to-value**; case narratives (e.g. large services firm **OCI** HR modernisation) cite **recruitment performance** and **payroll cycle** gains, raising the bar for **integrated** stories.

**Key statistics (mandatory)**

- HR tech **2025–2034** growth commonly quoted in **high single digits** (publisher variance).  
- **~72,000** fresher hiring headline across three IT majors (FY26 press summaries).  
- RPO market **~INR 8,000–9,000 crore** with **~18–20% CAGR** (consulting trade article).

## 03. Social factor

**Messaging and candidate communication**

- India is frequently described as **WhatsApp’s largest market** (hundreds of millions of users in popular summaries). Recruitment marketing literature claims **60–98%** message open rates versus **~20–25%** email; treat as **directional**.  
- **Mobile-first** behaviour aligns with India’s **billion-scale** mobile user base (Statista and telecom narratives; precise 2026 smartphone counts often paywalled).

**Skills, Gen AI, and assessment integrity**

- **LinkedIn**-reported surveys cited in Indian press claim **~75%** of recruiters investing heavily in **AI/tech hiring tools** and majorities using **analytics**; **Gen AI job titles** are growing sharply in secondary articles.  
- Social trust risk: trade press and verification vendors describe **resume fraud** (false employers, forged payslips, diploma mills) and **deepfake** interview attempts; **Gartner-style** warnings (via secondary reporting) frame **synthetic identity** as a rising C-suite concern.

**Inclusion and non-discrimination**

- **Supreme Court** intervention (**Jane Kaushik** line of cases, **October 2025** commentary) and **Delhi Transgender Protection Rules, 2025** increase expectations for **equal opportunity** policies and grievance mechanisms in **private** establishments, not only public sector.

**Key statistics (mandatory)**

- **Female LFPR** narratives in **PLFS** commentary: approximately **40%** annual female LFPR in one 2025 summary, with urban/rural splits; Economic Survey cites longer-run improvement.  
- Recruiter AI investment: **~75%** of Indian recruiters per **LinkedIn**-cited surveys (validate per customer).  
- Resume discrepancy rates (**~30%+** in vendor content): **directional** only.

## 04. Technological factor

**Cloud, SaaS, and HCM platforms**

- India **HCM** market commentary cites **low-double-digit CAGR** through early 2030s in one industry report; enterprises emphasise **cloud payroll**, **mobile ESS**, and **compliance automation**.

**AI in hiring**

- Recruiters report broad **AI tool** adoption (see Social factor); vendors and enterprises are experimenting with **matching, screening, scheduling, and interview analytics**.  
- **Deepfake** and **synthetic media** defences are moving from niche vendor stories toward **expected** enterprise diligence.

**Identity and verification tech**

- **Offline Aadhaar verification** and regulated **authentication** paths remain central to many KYC designs; **UIDAI** FAQs describe offline verification as usable broadly but **governance** still matters for storage and display.  
- Background-verification platforms emphasise **API-led** education and employment checks; **DPDP** raises the bar on **consent** and **processor** terms.

**Intermediary and synthetic-media rules**

- MeitY **October 2025** amendments to **IT Rules 2021** introduce **labelling** and **declaration** duties for **synthetically generated** content on intermediaries above thresholds, with **visibility** requirements for labels (e.g. minimum screen coverage in summaries). This is not an “ATS law” but shapes how **candidate-submitted media** may propagate.

**Key findings**

- **AI plus verification** stacks are becoming **table stakes** for Indian enterprises with **BFSI**-grade risk.  
- **Cloud** wins when statutory and payroll **data loops** close; recruiting is pulled into **platform** decisions.

## 06. Legal factor

### 6.1 India: DPDP Act 2023 and DPDP Rules 2025

- **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** establishes **Data Fiduciary** obligations, **Data Principal** rights, **consent** (or legitimate uses where enumerated), **notice**, **accuracy**, **security safeguards**, **breach** intimation to Board and principals, **children’s** data rules, and **Significant Data Fiduciary (SDF)** additional duties (DPO in India, **DPIA**-style assessments, periodic audit) under **Section 10** themes.  
- **DPDP Rules, 2025** (Ministry advisory summaries reference **November 2025** notification; **PIB** and **MeitY** explanatory materials circulated) operationalise **consent managers**, **verification** for children/disabilities, **cross-border** transfer procedures, and **enforcement** mechanics; commentary widely cites an **18-month** runway for full applicability from Rules notification (calendar **~May 2027** in advisory math).  
- **Cross-border transfers (Rule 15 / Section 16 framing):** summaries describe a **default permissive** posture unless **Central Government** prohibits specified jurisdictions or cases (**negative list** narrative), unlike GDPR adequacy-only models.  
- **Penalties (Section 33 schedules):** advisory tables cite **up to ₹200 crore** for several breaches (including security safeguards and breach notification failures), **up to ₹50 crore** for **DPIA/audit** failures, **up to ₹10 crore** for **DPO/contact** failures, and **up to ₹250 crore** for failure to comply with **Board** directions (exact mapping should be **legal-verified** against gazette text).  
- **Background verification:** employers remain **fiduciaries**; vendors are typically **processors** requiring **contractual** DPDP alignment; **purpose-limited consent** and **retention minimisation** are recurring advisory themes (some blogs cite **180-day** style defaults; treat as **non-authoritative** until confirmed against customer legal counsel).

### 6.2 EU GDPR (overlay for India-based processing of EU data subjects)

Relevant **GDPR (Regulation EU 2016/679)** articles for recruiting:

| Article | Recruiting relevance |
|--------|----------------------|
| **5** | Principles: lawfulness, fairness, transparency, minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity, accountability. |
| **6** | Lawful basis for processing (contract, consent, legitimate interests with LIA). |
| **9** | Special categories; explicit consent or employment law pathways for sensitive recruiting data. |
| **13–14** | Transparency and privacy notice content for candidates. |
| **17** | Erasure; ties to candidate purge and retention. |
| **20** | Data portability for candidate exports. |
| **22** | Rights related to **solely automated** decisions with legal/similarly significant effects; requires **human intervention**, explanation, and challenge pathways where applicable. |
| **35** | **DPIA** for high-risk processing (AI screening, systematic monitoring). |
| **44–50** | **Cross-border transfers** (SCCs, BCRs, adequacy). |
| **83** | **Administrative fines** up to **€20M or 4%** of global annual turnover for certain infringements. |

**Primary read:** [GDPR Article 22 (gdpr-info.eu)](https://gdpr-info.eu/art-22-gdpr/) (exceptions where decision is necessary for contract, authorised by law with safeguards, or based on **explicit consent**).

### 6.3 EU AI Act (overlay for EU deployers and EU-market AI systems)

- **Annex III(4)(a)** lists high-risk AI intended for **recruitment or selection**, including **targeted job ads**, **filtering applications**, and **evaluating candidates** ([EU AI Act Annex III explorer](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/annex/3/)).  
- **Article 9** – **risk management system**; **Article 10** – **data and data governance**; **Article 11** – **technical documentation**; **Article 12** – **record-keeping**; **Article 13** – **transparency to deployers**; **Article 14** – **human oversight**; **Article 27** – **fundamental rights impact assessment** for deployers in defined cases.  
- **Timeline:** industry summaries flag **August 2026** as a key enforcement milestone for high-risk obligations (note potential legislative **omnibus** proposals; monitor official Journal position).

### 6.4 Employment and equality adjacency

- **Transgender** workplace equality mandates (Supreme Court directions and **2025** rules in some states) increase **policy, grievance, and data-handling** care for gender-identity data.  
- **IT Rules 2025** synthetic-media duties may intersect with **candidate video** storage and redistribution policies.

**Key findings**

- India’s **DPDP** regime is **enforceability-forward** with **large fines** and **Board** powers; recruiting stacks must show **consent artefacts**, **notices**, **retention**, **sub-processor governance**, and **breach playbooks**.  
- **Global** accounts need **parallel** compliance with **GDPR** and **EU AI Act** for **AI matching, screening, and scheduling**, even when hiring occurs in India.

Workday must ship **granular, withdrawable consent and notice** for India **candidates and employees**, with **role-separated** visibility for **controllers vs processors**, **retention schedules** per data category, and **cross-border** transfer documentation that tracks **India’s negative-list** evolution plus **GDPR Chapter V** instruments for EU subjects. **AI-assisted ranking, matching, and interview workflows** should embed **human review** defaults, **audit logs**, and **explainability** artefacts to satisfy **GDPR Article 22** and **EU AI Act Articles 9–14 and 27** for European deployers, while **vendor-led background and identity checks** ride under **Article 28-style** processing terms aligned to **DPDP** fiduciary liability. **Aadhaar** and **biometric-adjacent** flows demand **minimisation** and **lawful purpose** discipline to avoid **over-collection** flagged by UIDAI and DPDP narratives alike.
